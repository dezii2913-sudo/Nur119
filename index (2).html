Fractions Basics

Fractions are numbers expressed as a numerator (top) over a denominator (bottom).

Types of Fractions:

  • Proper fraction: Numerator < denominator (e.g., 3/4)
  • Improper fraction: Numerator ‚â• denominator (e.g., 5/3)
  • Mixed number: Whole number + fraction (e.g., 1 ¬Ω)

Converting Mixed Numbers to Improper Fractions:

2 ¬æ = (2 √ó 4 + 3) / 4 = 11/4

üìè Metric Conversions

The Metric Ladder

The metric system is based on powers of 10. Moving DOWN the ladder (larger ‚Üí smaller) means multiplying by 1000. Moving UP means dividing by 1000.

From To Multiply/Divide Example
kg g √ó 1000 2 kg = 2000 g
g mg √ó 1000 1 g = 1000 mg
mg mcg √ó 1000 1 mg = 1000 mcg
L mL √ó 1000 1 L = 1000 mL
‚úÖ Memory Trick: Going DOWN = MULTIPLY. Going UP = DIVIDE.

Big Jump: Kilograms to Milligrams

Converting kg to mg requires going down TWO levels on the ladder, which creates very large numbers. Here are easier ways to handle it:

Method 1: Two-Step Approach (Easiest!)

Example: Convert 2 kg to mg

Step 1: kg ‚Üí g (multiply by 1000)
2 kg √ó 1000 = 2,000 g

Step 2: g ‚Üí mg (multiply by 1000 again)
2,000 g √ó 1000 = 2,000,000 mg

Method 2: Scientific Notation

1 kg = 1 √ó 10‚Å∂ mg (or 1,000,000 mg)

Quick Rule: Move decimal 6 places to the right
2 kg = 2.000000 ‚Üí 2,000,000 mg
0.5 kg = 0.500000 ‚Üí 500,000 mg

Method 3: Direct Multiplication

kg to mg: Multiply by 1,000,000 (or 10‚Å∂)

2 kg √ó 1,000,000 = 2,000,000 mg
üí° Best Practice: Use the two-step approach to avoid errors with large numbers. It's easier to multiply by 1000 twice than by 1,000,000 once!
‚ö†Ô∏è In Nursing Practice: You'll rarely need kg‚Üímg conversions directly. Most calculations use kg‚Üíg or g‚Üímg individually. But if you do need it, break it into two steps!

üîÑ Double Conversions Without Dimensional Analysis

Sometimes you need to convert through TWO units to get your answer. Here's the simple step-by-step method:

üîë Golden Rule: Convert one step at a time, then use your final converted number in the dosage formula!

Example 1: kg ‚Üí g ‚Üí mg (Weight Conversions)

Problem: Patient weighs 0.75 kg. Order is 20 mg/kg. How many mg total?

Step 1: Convert kg ‚Üí g
0.75 kg √ó 1000 = 750 g

Step 2: Convert g ‚Üí mg
750 g √ó 1000 = 750,000 mg

Step 3: Calculate dose
BUT WAIT! The order is 20 mg/kg, so:
0.75 kg √ó 20 mg/kg = 15 mg total dose

Note: You convert the weight to match what the order specifies!

Example 2: L ‚Üí mL ‚Üí drops (Volume to Drops)

Problem: 1.5 L IV fluid with 10 gtt/mL tubing. How many total drops?

Step 1: Convert L ‚Üí mL
1.5 L √ó 1000 = 1500 mL

Step 2: Convert mL ‚Üí drops using drop factor
1500 mL √ó 10 gtt/mL = 15,000 drops

Example 3: oz ‚Üí mL ‚Üí L (Household to Metric)

Problem: Patient drank 32 oz of water. Convert to liters.

Step 1: Convert oz ‚Üí mL
32 oz √ó 30 mL/oz = 960 mL

Step 2: Convert mL ‚Üí L
960 mL √∑ 1000 = 0.96 L

Example 4: lbs ‚Üí kg ‚Üí g (Weight Down Two Levels)

Problem: Child weighs 44 lbs. Medication requires dose in grams per kg. Convert to grams.

Step 1: Convert lbs ‚Üí kg
44 lbs √∑ 2.2 = 20 kg

Step 2: Convert kg ‚Üí g
20 kg √ó 1000 = 20,000 g

Use 20 kg for your calculation if order is per kg!

Example 5: mg ‚Üí g + Dosage Calculation (Most Common!)

Problem: Order: 750 mg PO | Available: 0.25 g/tablet

Step 1: Convert ordered dose to match available
750 mg √∑ 1000 = 0.75 g

Step 2: Calculate tablets needed
Tablets = Ordered √∑ Available
Tablets = 0.75 g √∑ 0.25 g = 3 tablets

Example 6: mcg ‚Üí mg ‚Üí mL (Dose + Volume)

Problem: Order: 500 mcg IV | Available: 2 mg/5 mL

Step 1: Convert ordered dose mcg ‚Üí mg
500 mcg √∑ 1000 = 0.5 mg

Step 2: Calculate mL needed
mL = (Ordered √ó Volume) √∑ Available
mL = (0.5 mg √ó 5 mL) √∑ 2 mg
mL = 2.5 √∑ 2 = 1.25 mL

Example 7: Hours ‚Üí Minutes for IV Calculation

Problem: 250 mL over 2.5 hours, drop factor 15 gtt/mL. Find gtt/min.

Step 1: Convert hours ‚Üí minutes
2.5 hours √ó 60 = 150 minutes

Step 2: Calculate gtt/min
gtt/min = (Volume √ó Drop Factor) √∑ Time
gtt/min = (250 √ó 15) √∑ 150
gtt/min = 3750 √∑ 150 = 25 gtt/min

‚Ä¢ Going bigger: Divide (mg‚Üíg, mL‚ÜíL, min‚Üíhr)

What IS Dimensional Analysis?

Dimensional Analysis (DA) is a problem-solving method that uses unit cancellation. It's your foolproof roadmap that works for EVERY dosage calculation problem - from simple tablets to complex critical care drips.

üéØ Why Your Instructors Love This Method:
‚úÖ Works for simple AND complex problems
‚úÖ Self-checking (if units don't cancel, setup is wrong)
‚úÖ No need to memorize 10 different formulas
‚úÖ Reduces decimal placement errors
‚úÖ Shows your work clearly for partial credit

The 4-Step Framework

  1. Step 1: Set up what you're solving for (LEFT side as a fraction)
    Example: mL/1 = ? or tablets/1 = ?
  2. Step 2: Write available information (RIGHT side - match numerator)
    If solving for mL, first fraction should have mL in numerator
    Example: mL/1 = (5 mL/250 mg) √ó ...
  3. Step 3: Add conversions so units cancel
    Add fractions until unwanted units cancel out
    Example: √ó (1000 mg/1 g) to convert g to mg
  4. Step 4: Add ordered dose and solve
    Add the ordered amount, cancel units, calculate
üîë GOLDEN RULE:
If units don't cancel to give you what you're solving for, you set it up WRONG!
Go back and flip a fraction upside down.

Visual Guide to Unit Cancellation

Example setup:

mL        5 mL        1000 mg       0.5 g
‚îÄ‚îÄ‚îÄ  =  ‚îÄ‚îÄ‚îÄ‚îÄ‚îÄ‚îÄ‚îÄ‚îÄ  √ó  ‚îÄ‚îÄ‚îÄ‚îÄ‚îÄ‚îÄ‚îÄ‚îÄ  √ó  ‚îÄ‚îÄ‚îÄ‚îÄ‚îÄ‚îÄ‚îÄ

Units that cancel: mg, g
Units remaining: mL ‚úì

How to cancel:

  • If a unit appears in BOTH numerator and denominator, it cancels
  • Cross out matched pairs as you go
  • What's left should be what you're solving for

üìñ Dimensional Analysis: Complete Examples

Example 1: Oral Dosage (Tablets)

Problem:

  • Order: Levothyroxine 0.05 mg PO every morning
  • Available: Levothyroxine 25 mcg tablets
  • Question: How many tablets will be administered?

Method 1: Dimensional Analysis

Step 1: What are we solving for?
tablets/1 = ?

Step 2: Write available (match tablets in numerator)
tablets/1 = (1 tablet/25 mcg) √ó ...

Step 3: Add conversion (mg to mcg)
tablets/1 = (1 tablet/25 mcg) √ó (1000 mcg/1 mg) √ó ...

Step 4: Add ordered dose
tablets/1 = (1 tablet/25 mcg) √ó (1000 mcg/1 mg) √ó (0.05 mg/1)

Cancel units: mcg cancels, mg cancels
tablets = (1 √ó 1000 √ó 0.05) √∑ (25 √ó 1 √ó 1)
tablets = 50 √∑ 25 = 2 tablets

Method 2: Simple Way (Desire/Have Formula)

üí° Quick Shortcut: Convert FIRST, then use simple formula
Step 1: Convert ordered dose to same units as available
0.05 mg √ó 1000 = 50 mcg

Step 2: Use formula: tablets = Ordered √∑ Available
tablets = 50 mcg √∑ 25 mcg = 2 tablets
Why this works: Once everything is in the same units, just divide what you need by what you have!

Example 2: Parenteral Dosage (IM Injection)

Problem:

  • Order: Penicillin G 200 mg IM now
  • Available: 0.5 g/2 mL
  • Question: How many mL will the nurse administer?

Method 1: Dimensional Analysis

Setup:
mL/1 = (2 mL/0.5 g) √ó (1 g/1000 mg) √ó (200 mg/1)

Cancel: g cancels (top & bottom), mg cancels
mL = (2 √ó 1 √ó 200) √∑ (0.5 √ó 1000 √ó 1)
mL = 400 √∑ 500 = 0.8 mL

Method 2: Simple Way

Step 1: Convert ordered to same units
200 mg √∑ 1000 = 0.2 g

Step 2: Set up proportion
If 0.5 g = 2 mL, then 0.2 g = ? mL

Step 3: Cross multiply
0.5 √ó ? = 2 √ó 0.2
? = 0.4 √∑ 0.5 = 0.8 mL
Or even simpler: mL = (Ordered √ó Volume) √∑ Available
mL = (0.2 g √ó 2 mL) √∑ 0.5 g = 0.4 √∑ 0.5 = 0.8 mL
‚úì Check: 0.8 mL is reasonable for IM injection (max is 3 mL)

Example 3: IV Flow Rate (Gravity - gtt/min)

Problem:

  • Order: D5W 150 mL over 6 hours IV
  • Available: Tubing drop factor 60 gtt/mL
  • Question: How many gtt/min will the IV deliver?
Note: When solving for gtt/min, you MUST have the tubing drop factor

Method 1: Dimensional Analysis

Setup:
gtt/min = (60 gtt/1 mL) √ó (150 mL/6 hr) √ó (1 hr/60 min)

Cancel: mL cancels, hr cancels
gtt/min = (60 √ó 150 √ó 1) √∑ (1 √ó 6 √ó 60)
gtt/min = 9000 √∑ 360 = 25
Answer: 25 gtt/min (round to whole number - can't count partial drops!)

Method 2: Simple Formula

Formula: gtt/min = (Volume √ó Drop Factor) √∑ Time in minutes

Step 1: Convert hours to minutes
6 hours √ó 60 = 360 minutes

Step 2: Plug into formula
gtt/min = (150 √ó 60) √∑ 360
gtt/min = 9000 √∑ 360 = 25 gtt/min
üí° Quick Tip: Memorize this formula - it's the fastest way for gravity IV!

Example 4: IV Flow Rate (Pump - mL/hr with mcg/min)

Problem:

  • Order: Nitroglycerin at 20 mcg/min
  • Available: 100 mg in 200 mL D5W
  • Question: What rate (mL/hr) should the IV pump be programmed?
Setup:
mL/hr = (200 mL/100 mg) √ó (1 mg/1000 mcg) √ó (20 mcg/min) √ó (60 min/1 hr)

Cancel: mg cancels, mcg cancels, min cancels
mL/hr = (200 √ó 1 √ó 20 √ó 60) √∑ (100 √ó 1000 √ó 1 √ó 1)
mL/hr = 240,000 √∑ 100,000 = 2.4 mL/hr

Example 5: Weight-Based IV (Solving for mL/hr)

Problem:

  • Order: Dopamine 3 mcg/kg/min
  • Available: 800 mg in 250 mL of 0.9% NS
  • Patient weight: 70 kg
  • Question: What mL/hr will you program the pump?
‚ö†Ô∏è CRITICAL WEIGHT RULE:
When solving for mL/hr, MULTIPLY by weight ‚Üí place as kg/1
Setup:
mL/hr = (250 mL/800 mg) √ó (1 mg/1000 mcg) √ó (3 mcg/kg/min) √ó (60 min/hr) √ó (70 kg/1)

Cancel: mg, mcg, kg, min all cancel
mL/hr = (250 √ó 1 √ó 3 √ó 60 √ó 70) √∑ (800 √ó 1000 √ó 1 √ó 1 √ó 1)
mL/hr = 3,150,000 √∑ 800,000 = 3.9 mL/hr

Example 6: Weight-Based IV (Solving for mcg/kg/min)

Problem:

  • Given: Patient receiving dopamine at 12 mL/hr
  • Concentration: 200 mg in 250 mL of 0.9% NS
  • Patient weight: 70 kg
  • Question: How many mcg/kg/min is patient receiving?
‚ö†Ô∏è CRITICAL WEIGHT RULE:
When solving for mcg/kg/min, DIVIDE by weight ‚Üí place as 1/kg
Setup:
mcg/kg/min = (1000 mcg/1 mg) √ó (200 mg/250 mL) √ó (12 mL/hr) √ó (1 hr/60 min) √ó (1/70 kg)

Cancel: mg, mL, hr all cancel
mcg/kg/min = (1000 √ó 200 √ó 12 √ó 1 √ó 1) √∑ (1 √ó 250 √ó 1 √ó 60 √ó 70)
mcg/kg/min = 2,400,000 √∑ 1,050,000 = 2.3 mcg/kg/min

üéØ Key Takeaways for Dimensional Analysis

  • Always start with what you're solving for on the left as a fraction
  • Match units vertically - solving for mL? First fraction on right has mL in numerator
  • Add conversions until all unwanted units cancel
  • Weight-based critical care:
    • Solving for mL/hr? Weight = kg/1 (you MULTIPLY by weight)
    • Solving for mcg/kg/min? Weight = 1/kg (you DIVIDE by weight)
  • If units don't cancel correctly, flip a fraction!
  • Always check your answer makes sense (0.0001 mL or 500 tablets = wrong!)

üß™ Reconstitution & Units Dosing

Reconstitution of Powdered Medications

Some medications come as powders that must be mixed with a diluent (usually sterile water or normal saline) before administration.

Why Powders?

  • Drug is unstable in liquid form
  • Extends shelf life
  • Allows for multiple concentration options

Reconstitution Steps:

  1. Read the label carefully - Note required diluent amount and final concentration
  2. Add exact amount of diluent - Follow package directions precisely
  3. Mix gently - Roll or swirl (don't shake vigorously)
  4. Label the vial - Date, time, concentration, YOUR initials
  5. Use NEW concentration for your calculation
  6. Check expiration - Reconstituted meds expire faster!
‚ö†Ô∏è CRITICAL: After reconstitution, the volume may be GREATER than diluent added due to powder displacement. Always use the FINAL concentration listed on the label!

Gravity IV Formula (gtt/min)

gtt/min = (Total Volume √ó Drop Factor) √∑ (Total Time in minutes)

Example:

Order: 1000 mL D5W over 8 hours
Tubing: 15 gtt/mL

Step 1: Convert hours to minutes: 8 hr √ó 60 = 480 min
Step 2: gtt/min = (1000 mL √ó 15 gtt/mL) √∑ 480 min
Step 3: gtt/min = 15,000 √∑ 480 = 31.25
Step 4: Round to whole number = 31 gtt/min
Always round gtt/min to WHOLE NUMBER - you can't count partial drops!

IV Pump Formula (mL/hr)

mL/hr = Total Volume (mL) √∑ Total Time (hours)

Example:

Order: 1000 mL NS over 10 hours

mL/hr = 1000 mL √∑ 10 hr = 100 mL/hr

That's it! IV pump calculations are usually straightforward.

Infusion Time Calculations

Sometimes you need to find how long an IV will take to infuse.

Time (hr) = Total Volume (mL) √∑ Rate (mL/hr)

Example:

Given: 500 mL infusing at 75 mL/hr
Question: How many hours will it take?

Time = 500 mL √∑ 75 mL/hr = 6.67 hours
= 6 hours and 40 minutes

Heparin Protocols

Heparin requires weight-based dosing with frequent monitoring via aPTT (activated partial thromboplastin time).

Typical Heparin Protocol:

  1. Bolus: 80 units/kg IV push
  2. Continuous infusion: 18 units/kg/hr
  3. Check aPTT in 6 hours
  4. Adjust per protocol:
    • aPTT < 35: Increase rate
    • aPTT 35-45: Increase rate slightly
    • aPTT 46-70 (therapeutic): Continue
    • aPTT 71-90: Decrease rate
    • aPTT > 90: Hold, recheck, restart lower
‚ö†Ô∏è Heparin Safety:
‚Ä¢ Two RN verification required
‚Ä¢ Use pump (never gravity)
‚Ä¢ Have protamine sulfate available (reversal agent)
‚Ä¢ Monitor for bleeding

üë∂ Pediatric Dosing

Why Pediatric Dosing Is Different

  • Immature organ systems - especially liver & kidneys
  • Different body composition - higher water content
  • Faster metabolism in some cases
  • Greater risk of toxicity - narrow therapeutic window
‚ö†Ô∏è ZERO TOLERANCE FOR ERROR!
Pediatric dosing errors can be fatal. ALWAYS:
‚Ä¢ Calculate safe dose range FIRST
‚Ä¢ Compare ordered dose to safe range
‚Ä¢ Question ANY dose outside safe range
‚Ä¢ Get second RN to verify

Weight Conversions

Pediatric doses are based on weight in kilograms.

Pounds to Kilograms:

kg = lbs √∑ 2.2

Example:

Infant weighs 24 lbs

kg = 24 √∑ 2.2 = 10.9 kg

Grams to Kilograms (Neonates):

kg = grams √∑ 1000

Example:

Neonate weighs 2300 g

kg = 2300 √∑ 1000 = 2.3 kg

Safe Dose Range Verification

Before calculating ANY pediatric dose, verify it's safe!

Steps:

  1. Look up safe dose range in drug reference
  2. Calculate minimum safe dose (lower limit √ó kg)
  3. Calculate maximum safe dose (upper limit √ó kg)
  4. Compare ordered dose to safe range
  5. If outside range: QUESTION THE ORDER!
  6. If within range: Calculate dose to give

Example:

Patient: 15 kg child
Order: Amoxicillin 300 mg PO TID
Safe range: 20-40 mg/kg/day divided q8h

Min safe: 20 mg/kg/day √ó 15 kg = 300 mg/day
Max safe: 40 mg/kg/day √ó 15 kg = 600 mg/day
Ordered: 300 mg √ó 3 = 900 mg/day
900 mg > 600 mg ‚Üí UNSAFE! Call provider!

Once you have the 24-hour amount, divide by 24 to get mL/hr for pump setting.

Example:

1250 mL √∑ 24 hr = 52.1 mL/hr ‚Üí Set pump at 52 mL/hr

ü§∞ Obstetric Dosages (Oxytocin)

Oxytocin (Pitocin)

Oxytocin is used to induce or augment labor. It's a HIGH-ALERT medication!

‚ö†Ô∏è HIGH-ALERT OB MEDICATION!
Can cause:
‚Ä¢ Uterine hyperstimulation
‚Ä¢ Fetal distress
‚Ä¢ Uterine rupture
‚Ä¢ Water intoxication

Oxytocin Dosing

  • Measured in: milliunits per minute (mU/min)
  • Conversion: 1 unit = 1000 milliunits
  • Typical concentration: 10-40 units in 1000 mL
  • Start dose: 0.5-2 mU/min
  • Titrate: Increase 1-2 mU/min every 30 min
  • Max dose: Usually 20-40 mU/min

Typical Oxytocin Order

Order:

  • 1000 mL D5LR + 20 units oxytocin
  • Start at 1 mU/min
  • Increase by 1 mU/min every 30 min PRN
  • Maximum 20 mU/min
  • Goal: Contractions every 2-3 min lasting 60-90 sec

Safety Monitoring:

  • Continuous fetal heart rate monitoring
  • Uterine contraction monitoring
  • Maternal vital signs q15-30min
  • I&O (watch for fluid overload)
